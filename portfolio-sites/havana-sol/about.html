Nuestra historia

One family, one suitcase of recipes, fifteen years on Calle del Sol

We didn’t set out to build a restaurant. We set out to cook the food we missed, for anyone who’d pull up a chair.

2009Where it began

How we started

It started with a borrowed espresso pot

Mireya and Tomás Cordero arrived in Tampa from Santiago de Cuba with two children, a stack of handwritten recipes, and not much else. Tomás found work as a line cook; Mireya sold croquetas and cafecito out of a ventanita window on weekends until the line down the block made the decision for them.

In 2009 they signed the lease on a tired storefront in the Riverside Arts District. They painted the door teal, hung Tomás’s father’s guitar on the wall, and opened with eleven tables and one rule: nothing leaves the kitchen that we wouldn’t serve our own family.

That rule hasn’t changed. Almost everything else has grown.

A few good years

The road from there to here

2009

We open the door

Eleven tables, one plancha, and Abuela Mireya’s flan recipe taped inside the walk-in. We sold out of lechón on the first Saturday.

2013

The music starts

A trio of regulars asked to play for tips one Friday. They never really left, and live son became part of who we are.

2016

Best Cuban in the bay

Bay Weekly readers voted us a top Cuban kitchen in Tampa. We framed it next to the guitar.

2019

Lucía takes the kitchen

The Corderos’ daughter, trained in Havana and Madrid, became head chef, keeping the old recipes and adding a few of her own.

2024

Still family-run

A second dining room, a full bar, a catering team, and the same teal door. Tomás still pulls the first cafecito every morning.

Meet the chef

Lucía Cordero cooks with one foot in two cities

Lucía grew up doing homework at table four and peeling plantains after school. She trained formally in Havana, then spent four years in Madrid kitchens learning technique, before coming home to run the family pass.

Her philosophy is simple: respect the recipe, source it better, and don’t over-think it. The mojo is still her grandmother’s. The snapper is line-caught from the Gulf. The flan has never changed and never will.

“People think Cuban food is heavy,” she says. “It’s not. It’s generous. There’s a difference, and you can taste it.”

Cómo cocinamos

The traditions we cook by

Cuban cooking is a conversation between Spain, Africa, and the Caribbean. These are the threads we never cut.

01

El sofrito

Everything starts in the same pan: onion, garlic, peppers, and cumin cooked slow until sweet. It’s the foundation of half our menu and the smell of every Cuban childhood.

02

El mojo

Sour orange, garlic, and oregano, squeezed and pounded by hand every morning. It marinates the pork, dresses the yuca, and finishes the fish. Never from a bottle.

03

La plancha

The flat-top press that gives the cubano its crackle and the vaca frita its crisp edges. Ours has been seasoned by fifteen years of service and we’ll never replace it.

04

El tiempo

Time. Lechón roasts for eight hours, oxtail for three, beans overnight. Good Cuban food cannot be rushed, and we don’t try.

05

El café

A meal isn’t finished without a cafecito, and a cafecito is meant to be shared. The ventanita window pours from open to close.

06

La música

Son, bolero, and a little salsa. In Cuba, food and music live at the same table. On weekends, so do they here.
