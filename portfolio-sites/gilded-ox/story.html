Our Story

Fire, time, and a room worth keeping

A chophouse built inside a nineteenth-century gallery, where the cooking is patient and the walls are worth the visit on their own.

The Idea

A great steak deserves a great room

We opened The Gilded Ox with a simple conviction. The best steakhouses are not only about the beef. They are about the room you eat it in, the people who pour your wine, and the unhurried hours you give to the evening.

So we found a building with a hundred years in its bones, restored it by hand, and filled it with the art and the fire we love. Everything since has followed from that.

The Building

From picture gallery to chophouse

1887

The gallery rises

Built on Peachtree Road as a private picture gallery for the Hartwell family, lit by tall north windows and lined in carved oak.

1929

A long quiet

The original collection is sold at auction. The rooms pass through a dozen tenants across the next eight decades.

2012

The restoration

Eleanor Vance acquires the building and begins a two-year restoration of plaster cornices, heart-pine floors, and that north light.

2014

The Gilded Ox opens

Chef Soren Whitfield fires the hearth for the first time. The walls are hung. The first porterhouse leaves the pass.

2024

Recognition

Named a James Beard semifinalist for Outstanding Hospitality, and the cellar earns a Wine Spectator Award of Excellence.

The Collection

Worth the visit on their own

Our collection reaches back to the Dutch masters and forward through the Romantics. A selection hangs in the dining rooms; the rest rotates through the private salons.

The People

Two obsessions, one table

Soren Whitfield, Executive Chef. Trained in Lyon and seasoned across a decade of New York steakhouses, Soren built our dry-age cellar by hand and tends the hearth most nights himself.

Eleanor Vance, Proprietor. A collector first and a restaurateur second, Eleanor spent twenty years gathering the paintings before she found the room they deserved.

How We Cook

Three things we will not rush

01

Fire

Everything of consequence passes over white oak and lump charcoal. No gas, no shortcuts, just smoke and a careful hand on the grill.

02

Time

We dry-age whole primals from twenty-eight to sixty days in a cellar beneath the dining room, until the flavor turns deep and nutty.

03

The Room

Candlelight, pressed linen, unhurried service, and original art on every wall. The setting is half the meal, and we treat it that way.
