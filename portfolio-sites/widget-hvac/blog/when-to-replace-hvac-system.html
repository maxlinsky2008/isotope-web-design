Repair or Replace? How to Decide When Your HVAC System Is Failing

A failing system forces a decision. Here's the framework we use to give homeowners an honest answer — not the one that earns us more.

The Age Factor

Age is the starting point for every repair-or-replace conversation. General useful-life benchmarks:

  • Central air conditioners: 15–20 years
  • Gas furnaces: 20–30 years (with proper maintenance)
  • Heat pumps: 15–20 years
  • Boilers: 20–35 years

In Atlanta, these lifespans trend toward the shorter end. Our systems run 8–10 months of the year, humidity accelerates corrosion, and high summer demand strains equipment. A 15-year-old AC in Atlanta has worked harder than a 15-year-old AC in Portland.

The 50% Rule

The most reliable decision framework: if the repair cost exceeds 50% of the replacement cost, and the system is more than 10 years old, replace it.

Example: Your 14-year-old AC needs a compressor replacement quoted at $2,200. A new system costs $5,800 installed. $2,200 is 38% of $5,800 — below 50%. But: the compressor is the heart of the system, and replacing it in a 14-year-old unit often just shifts the next failure to another component. We'd still lean toward replacement and tell you exactly why.

The 50% rule is a floor, not a ceiling. If the repair is cheap but the system has a documented history of repeated failures, that matters too. We track service history and will walk you through what we've seen.

The Refrigerant Question

Systems manufactured before 2010 commonly use R-22 refrigerant (sold under the brand name Freon). R-22 was phased out under the Montreal Protocol and is no longer manufactured in the US.

This matters because R-22 systems that develop refrigerant leaks face two bad options: expensive reclaimed R-22 (when it can be found), or conversion to a different refrigerant that the system wasn't designed for. Neither is a long-term solution.

If your system uses R-22 and is more than 10 years old, replacement planning should start now — not when the next leak forces your hand in July.

Current standard refrigerants are R-410A and the newer R-32/R-454B. All new systems we install use current-generation refrigerants.

Comfort and Efficiency Signals

Age and repair cost tell part of the story. These signals tell the rest:

  • Uneven temperatures: Rooms that won't stay at the set temperature despite a functioning system often indicate distribution problems or a system that's sized wrong for the current load.
  • Humidity control problems: An air conditioner removes humidity as a byproduct of cooling. One that's oversized (common after poorly planned replacements) short-cycles before removing enough humidity. Persistent sticky air is a system problem.
  • Rising utility bills: Compare this year's summer bills to last year's for the same months. A 15–20% unexplained increase in a system older than 10 years is a strong efficiency-decline signal.
  • Repeated failures in a single season: One repair might be bad luck. Two in a summer is a pattern. Three and you're spending repair money on borrowed time.

How We Present the Options

When we give you a repair quote, we tell you three things:

  1. The flat-rate repair cost
  2. Our honest estimate of what this repair buys you in additional system life
  3. What replacement would cost, and the projected energy savings at current utility rates

We make the same margin on a repair as a replacement. There's no financial reason for us to push you either direction, which is why we can be straight with you. Some repairs genuinely make sense. Some replacements are clearly overdue. We'll tell you which one you're looking at.
