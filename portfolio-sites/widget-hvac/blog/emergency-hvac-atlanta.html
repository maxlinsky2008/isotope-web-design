Your AC died at 9pm on a July Friday. We're on the way. Here's how to keep your home livable and your family safe until we get there.

Everything below assumes you've already made that call and we're on the way.

Immediate Steps When Your System Fails

1

Check the circuit breaker

HVAC systems draw high amperage and trip breakers during voltage spikes or component failures. Find your electrical panel, locate the breaker labeled "HVAC," "AC," "Air Handler," or "Furnace," and check if it's tripped (switched to center or off). Reset it once. If it trips again immediately, leave it off — a repeatedly tripping breaker indicates a real electrical problem and we need to diagnose it.

2

Set the thermostat to fan-only mode

Switch your thermostat to "fan on" (not "auto"). This circulates air through your home even without active cooling or heating, which helps manage temperature and keeps air moving. It won't cool or heat the space, but it makes a meaningful comfort difference in summer heat.

3

Check and replace a clogged filter

An extremely restricted air filter triggers safety shutoffs on many systems. Pull your filter and check it. If it's visibly clogged with dust and debris, replace it. A fresh filter is a quick fix that sometimes restores operation immediately — and if it does, it tells us something about your maintenance history.

4

Check the condensate drain line (summer AC failures)

During cooling season, a clogged condensate drain is one of the most common causes of sudden shutdowns. Your AC removes humidity from the air; that water has to drain somewhere. Find the drain line (usually a PVC pipe near your indoor air handler) and the float switch (a safety device that shuts the system off if water backs up). If you see standing water in the drain pan, the line is blocked. Clear it with a wet-dry vacuum on the drain outlet, or flush it with water.

5

Stay comfortable while you wait

In summer: close blinds and curtains to block solar heat gain. Move to the lowest floor of the house — it's naturally cooler. Use battery-powered fans if available. In winter: gather your household to one room and close doors. Body heat is meaningful in a small space. Dress in layers.

What Not to Do

Don't run the system if you smell burning. A burning smell — especially electrical burning — indicates a component failure that could cause a fire. Shut the system off at the thermostat and the breaker and don't restart it. Call us immediately.

  • Don't attempt refrigerant diagnosis yourself. Refrigerant is under high pressure and requires EPA certification to handle legally. If you suspect a refrigerant leak (ice on the copper lines, hissing sound, system running but not cooling), shut it off and wait for us.
  • Don't pour water on an overheating unit. An outdoor condenser in distress needs airflow and shade, not water. Hosing it down with cold water can cause thermal shock damage to the condenser coils.
  • Don't keep resetting a tripping breaker. A breaker that trips repeatedly is doing its job — protecting the circuit from a fault. Resetting it repeatedly works around that protection. Leave it off after the second trip.
  • Don't leave vulnerable people in dangerous heat. If indoor temperatures climb above 90°F and you have elderly relatives, infants, or anyone medically vulnerable, move them to an air-conditioned location — a neighbor's home, a cooling center, or a hotel — while you wait for service.
